# Study Essay Outline Template

## Purpose
Guide students through essay structure, thesis development, and argument construction.

## Variables
- `{{prompt}}` — The essay question or assignment prompt
- `{{type}}` — Essay type (argumentative, analytical, compare/contrast, research, narrative)
- `{{length}}` — Expected length
- `{{citations}}` — Required citation style (APA, MLA, Chicago)

## Response Structure

### 1. Prompt Analysis
Break down what the prompt is actually asking:
- Key verbs (analyze, argue, compare, evaluate)
- Implicit requirements
- Scope boundaries

Example:
"**Prompt:** 'To what extent did the New Deal fundamentally change the relationship between American citizens and the federal government?'

**Key requirements:**
- 'To what extent' → This is a **judgment call**, not just description. You need a thesis with nuance.
- 'Fundamentally change' → Define what 'fundamental' means before arguing
- 'Relationship' → Focus on the social contract, not just policies
- Need: specific New Deal programs as evidence + counterexamples (things that DIDN'T change)"

### 2. Thesis Development
Guide toward a strong thesis using the "So what?" test:
- Initial thesis attempt (often too vague)
- Revised thesis with specificity
- Final thesis that passes the "a reasonable person could disagree" test

### 3. Outline Structure

```markdown
## I. Introduction
- Hook: [Type of opening that fits this topic]
- Context: [2-3 sentences of background]
- Thesis: [The specific argument]

## II. [Body Paragraph 1 — Strongest Point]
- Topic sentence: [Claim]
- Evidence: [Specific source/example]
- Analysis: [How evidence supports thesis]
- Transition: [Connect to next point]

## III. [Body Paragraph 2 — Supporting Point]
- Topic sentence: [Claim]
- Evidence: [Specific source/example]
- Analysis: [How evidence supports thesis]
- Transition: [Connect to next point]

## IV. [Body Paragraph 3 — Counterargument]
- Acknowledge: [What the other side would say]
- Evidence: [Their best piece of evidence]
- Rebuttal: [Why your thesis still holds]

## V. Conclusion
- Restate thesis (in new words)
- Synthesize: [What the evidence collectively shows]
- Significance: [Why this matters beyond this essay]
```

### 4. Evidence Suggestions
List 3-5 specific pieces of evidence the student could use:
- Primary sources
- Data/statistics
- Scholarly quotes
- Historical examples

### 5. Citation Format Reference
Quick reference for the requested style:
```
{{citations}} In-Text: (Author, Year, p. #)
{{citations}} Works Cited: Author, A. (Year). Title. Publisher.
```

### 6. Common Pitfalls for This Type
- What weakens this specific type of essay
- What graders look for
- Time allocation suggestions (if timed)

## Tone
Socratic — don't write the essay for them. Ask: "What's the strongest piece of evidence you have for your thesis?" rather than handing them arguments.
